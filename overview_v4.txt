# Project Overview

---

## 📄 Title (working)

**The Masked Advantage: Disentangling Language Proficiency from Cultural Knowledge Access in Multilingual LLMs**

---

## 1️⃣ Motivation

Prior work on multilingual LLMs has reached an apparent contradiction. Studies of cross-lingual factual consistency (Qi et al., 2023; Wendler et al., 2024; Schut et al., 2025) show that LLMs internally pivot through English and that knowledge transfers asymmetrically across languages. Studies of cultural and geographic knowledge (Yin et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024; Pawar et al., 2025), however, report a counterintuitive finding: for English-centric models, English is often a *better* query language than the local language even for culturally-specific questions — content that is overwhelmingly attested in local-language corpora.

We argue this consensus rests on a measurement confound. Comparing raw accuracy across languages conflates two effects that can pull in opposite directions:

- **Language proficiency**: the model understands one language better than another.
- **Language-conditioned knowledge access**: localized knowledge is preferentially encoded in or retrievable through a particular language.

When these effects oppose each other, raw accuracy reflects only their net sign. Prior work has been reporting net signs as if they were direct measurements of knowledge access.

This work introduces a controlled framework that decomposes these two effects and applies it across diverse locales and model families. Our central hypothesis is that **a positive local-language knowledge advantage exists consistently across model types, but in English-centric models is masked at the surface by the proficiency penalty.**

The implications are practical as well as scientific: if true, multilingual users may face systematically different — and potentially worse — answers depending on which language they query in, in ways that are invisible to standard accuracy-based evaluation.

---

## 2️⃣ Research Questions

**RQ1 (Decomposition).** Does the local language carry a distinct knowledge advantage for culturally-specific questions once language proficiency is controlled for?

**RQ2 (When does local language win on raw performance?).** Under what conditions — which locales, which model families — does asking in the local language actually yield better cultural performance than asking in English? When the local-language advantage is present in knowledge access but masked by proficiency, when does it become large enough to surface in raw accuracy?

**RQ3 (Robustness across locales and models).** Is the knowledge gap consistent across different cultural and linguistic contexts? Do regional, global, and multilingual models exhibit systematically different patterns?

**RQ4 (Behavior under failure).** When knowledge access fails, does the language of the query shift the balance between abstention and confident error? Does querying in the local language expose users to more confident hallucinations than querying in English on the same underlying knowledge?

---

## 3️⃣ Methodology

### 3.1 Overview

We design a controlled evaluation framework that crosses two factors:

- **Question type**: culture-agnostic (CA) vs. culture-specific (CS).
- **Query language**: local language vs. English.

This 2×2 design supports a difference-in-differences decomposition: the language effect on CA questions isolates proficiency; the language effect on CS questions captures proficiency plus knowledge access; their contrast isolates knowledge access.

### 3.2 Dataset Construction

For each locale, we construct three subsets:

- **CA (culture-agnostic)**: drawn from Global-MMLU using its CA labels. Provides a baseline for measuring language proficiency on knowledge that does not depend on local context.
- **CS (culture-specific)**: drawn from native regional MMLU benchmarks (e.g., CMMLU, ArabicMMLU, KMMLU). We filter to questions tagged as culture-specific by the original authors, then have annotators verify that each question genuinely requires local knowledge to solve.
- **CS_EN**: CS questions translated to English via Claude, then human post-edited and back-translation-checked to confirm semantic preservation.

### 3.3 Locales

We cover **13 locales** spanning different resource levels, scripts, and regions: Chinese, Arabic, Greek, Hindi, Korean, French, Japanese, Bengali, Dutch, Nepali, Persian, Polish, Telugu.

This range allows us to study the language-conditioned knowledge effect across:
- **resource levels**: high-resource (Chinese, French) to low-resource (Telugu, Nepali, Bengali);
- **scripts**: Latin (French, Dutch, Polish), Arabic (Arabic, Persian), Devanagari (Hindi, Nepali), Greek, CJK (Chinese, Japanese), Hangul (Korean), Bengali, Telugu;
- **regions**: East Asia (Chinese, Japanese, Korean), South Asia (Hindi, Bengali, Nepali, Telugu), Middle East (Arabic, Persian), Europe (Greek, French, Dutch, Polish).

### 3.4 Models

We evaluate **approximately 80 model checkpoints** drawn from over 20 families, falling into three categories, with the global-model category further crossed by **country of origin** to disentangle "trained primarily on English" from "trained by a US lab." Every model carries a country-of-origin label, and where a family ships both a base (pretrained) and an instruction-tuned variant we evaluate both, so instruction tuning can be read as a within-family factor.

- **Globally-trained, English-centric**, organized by country of origin:
  - *USA*: Llama 3.1 / 3.2 (Meta) and Gemma 2 / 3 / 4 (Google), plus the proprietary frontier models GPT-5.4 and GPT-5.4-mini (OpenAI) and Gemini-3-Flash (Google).
  - *China*: Qwen 2.5 / 3 / 3.5 (Alibaba) and GLM-4 / GLM-4.7 (Zhipu).
  - *France*: Ministral / Mistral-Medium / Mistral-Small (Mistral) and Gaperon.

  We treat country of origin as a secondary dimension here because global models from different countries differ in their multilingual training mix and cultural emphasis even when English dominates.
- **Regionally-trained**: models built with strong local-language and cultural data, spanning the Middle East (Jais-2, AceGPT-v2 — UAE; Fanar-2 — Qatar), Greece (Llama-Krikri, Meltemi, Minerva), Korea (KORMo, Llama-3-Open-Ko), Japan (Qwen3-Swallow), Iran (PersianMind), Poland (Bielik), and Southeast Asia (SEA-LION, SeaLLM).
- **Multilingual / balanced**: models explicitly optimized for cross-lingual coverage — broadly multilingual (Aya-Expanse, Cohere) and European-multilingual (EuroLLM, Teuken).

This three-category design with the country-of-origin sub-axis lets us separate "trained on language X" from "trained in country Y" — two factors that prior work has often conflated.

### 3.5 Scoring and Metrics

All three headline gaps — GlobalGap, LocalGap, and KnowledgeGap — are computed from model-ability estimates fit by a **1PL (Rasch) IRT model**, not from raw accuracy. Classical accuracy runs into a chicken-and-egg problem: an item's difficulty distorts the ability we infer from it, while ability distorts our reading of difficulty. Even within a single subset, this matters: not all locales sample the same items in identical proportions, so a raw-accuracy comparison silently lets composition drift contaminate the language effect we are trying to measure. The Rasch model breaks this loop by separating item difficulty from model ability under a single likelihood.

**Model.** We model the probability that model j answers item i correctly as

P_i(θ_j) = σ(θ_j − b_i),

where θ is a per-model, per-subset, per-prompt-language ability and b is a per-item difficulty. Every logical item receives a single universal difficulty b_i shared across its translated facets, so the difficulty term isolates the underlying problem from the prompt language; each model receives one ability parameter θ_{j, CA/CS, language} per subset × prompt language cell. The response is binary correctness (y = 1 correct, y = 0 incorrect), and the model is fit jointly across all subsets.

**Fitting.** We fit by maximum likelihood rather than full Bayesian sampling: θ and b are free parameters with no prior, optimized by Adam (learning rate 0.05, up to 5000 steps with early stopping) to minimize the negative log-likelihood — equivalently, the binary cross-entropy on the logits θ_j − b_i. The 1PL likelihood is invariant under a joint location shift of θ and b, so after every optimization step we remove this degree of freedom with a hard anchor applied per connected component of the item–subset graph: the single CA component (whose items appear across all CA subsets) and one CS component per language (whose items are shared only between that language's CS and CS_EN subsets), each re-centered to mean-zero ability. Because the CA and CS components share no item, they are anchored on separate scales.

**Gaps as θ contrasts.** From the fitted ability estimates we compute:

- **GlobalGap** = θ_{CA, local} − θ_{CA, English} → language proficiency, on the CA component's common scale.
- **LocalGap** = θ_{CS, local} − θ_{CS, English} → proficiency + knowledge access, on each locale's CS component's common scale.
- **KnowledgeGap** = (θ_{CS, local} − θ_{CS, English}) − (θ_{CA, local} − θ_{CA, English}), standardized to account for the separately-anchored CA and CS scales.

All three gaps live in log-odds (logit) units rather than accuracy percentage points: a positive value means the model is more *able* on that subset in the local language than in English, after partialling out item difficulty. GlobalGap and LocalGap are within-component contrasts (the two items being compared are physically the same, in two prompt languages), so they sit on a jointly-anchored scale and need no further standardization. KnowledgeGap crosses the CA and CS components, which are anchored separately, so the contrast is standardized to make the cross-component comparison meaningful.

**Uncertainty.** MLE returns a single point estimate (θ̂, b̂) rather than a posterior, so we report all three gaps as point estimates. Attaching frequentist confidence intervals to any of them would require propagating the curvature of the log-likelihood at the optimum — the Fisher information and its Schur complement onto the θ contrasts of interest — which we leave to future work (see §6). Full specification — likelihood, optimization, anchoring, and identifiability — appears in the appendix.

Computing all three gaps under the same Rasch fit gives us a unified scale (log-odds ability) and lets item-difficulty differences across model–locale cells be accounted for everywhere, not only at the cross-component contrast. Raw-accuracy gaps remain reported alongside the headline θ gaps as a sanity check and to connect with prior work, but they are not the metrics our claims rest on.

### 3.6 Abstention and Hallucination Analysis (RQ4)

We extend the evaluation with a fifth option, E = "I don't know," and compute per cell:

- **accuracy** = correct / n
- **abstain rate** = abstained / n
- **confident error rate** = wrong-without-abstaining / n
- **conditional accuracy** = correct / (correct + confident wrong)

The key question is whether query language shifts the abstain-versus-confabulate balance — whether English-centric models, queried in the local language on CS questions, produce more confident errors than when queried in English. If so, this is a deployment-relevant finding: multilingual users may face systematically higher rates of confident hallucination on culturally-specific questions, and this rate depends predictably on locale and model type.

---

## 4️⃣ Current Findings

Our preliminary results across the 13 locales and the three model categories support the masked-advantage hypothesis. The three θ-based metrics tell a coherent story when read together.

**GlobalGap is uniformly negative.** Across all locales and all model types, models exhibit lower CA ability in the local language than in English. This is the proficiency baseline: even for knowledge that has nothing to do with local culture, English is the stronger query language. The effect is consistent and unambiguous on the log-odds scale.

**LocalGap is mixed and resource-dependent.** Ability on culture-specific questions, by contrast, varies systematically across locales. **Chinese is the only locale where LocalGap is consistently positive** — that is, querying in Chinese on Chinese cultural questions yields higher estimated CS ability than translating to English. We attribute this to Chinese being roughly on par with English in training-data scale and quality, which neutralizes the proficiency penalty and lets the local-language knowledge advantage surface in the within-CS θ contrast. For most other locales, LocalGap is negative or near zero, with the most negative values appearing for lower-resource locales. Given that CS_EN translations are imperfect, the true negative values are likely somewhat smaller in magnitude than we observe — translation noise inflates the apparent English disadvantage on CS_EN, which means our negative LocalGap estimates are conservative.

**KnowledgeGap is positive across the board.** Once we take the cross-component contrast that nets out the CA proficiency penalty, the knowledge gap δ is positive in nearly every (locale, model) cell. This is the central finding: **even when raw cultural performance is worse in the local language, the local language carries a measurable knowledge advantage that is hidden under the proficiency penalty.** The "English is better" appearance in raw accuracy is real at the surface but misleading as a claim about where cultural knowledge is encoded. We currently report δ as a point estimate; quantifying the uncertainty around it (see §6) is the natural next step to confirm how robustly each cell clears zero.

Read together, the three results form a clean decomposition:

- **GlobalGap (always negative)** — English wins on proficiency, full stop.
- **LocalGap (mixed)** — whether English still wins on cultural questions depends on whether the local language has enough resources to overcome its proficiency penalty.
- **KnowledgeGap (consistently positive)** — the local language has a real knowledge advantage everywhere; we just usually can't see it in raw accuracy because proficiency drags performance down.

Chinese is the natural test case where the proficiency drag is smallest, and it is exactly where the knowledge advantage breaks through to the surface — consistent with the framework's prediction.

## 5️⃣ Summary of Approach

This work makes three contributions:

1. **A measurement framework.** A DiD decomposition that separates language proficiency from language-conditioned knowledge access, with all three gaps — GlobalGap, LocalGap, KnowledgeGap — estimated as θ contrasts under a single 1PL (Rasch) IRT model fit by maximum likelihood. This unifies the within-component proficiency and language-effect measures with the cross-component knowledge contrast on one ability scale.
2. **A reframing of an active debate.** We show that the existing "is local language better?" question rests on a confound. By decomposing proficiency from knowledge access, we can ask both (a) whether the local language carries a knowledge advantage across model types — empirically, yes, nearly everywhere — and (b) when this advantage becomes large enough to win in raw performance — empirically, when the local language is resource-rich enough to close the proficiency gap, as Chinese illustrates.
3. **A deployment-relevant safety finding.** Querying in the local language may systematically increase confident hallucination on culturally-specific questions in English-centric models, with implications for how multilingual LLMs should be evaluated and deployed.

## 6️⃣ Next Steps (Beyond Current Scope)

The following extensions strengthen the work but are deferred from the initial submission to keep the scope focused:

**Uncertainty quantification for the θ gaps.** The MLE pipeline currently reports GlobalGap, LocalGap, and δ as single point estimates, with no interval. Two complementary routes recover uncertainty: (a) propagate the curvature of the log-likelihood at the optimum — the Fisher information and its Schur complement onto the θ contrasts — to attach frequentist confidence intervals to every gap (the construction documented for the binary 2PL MLE in `06_mle_schur_fisher_ci.md`); or (b) refit the same 1PL model under NUTS to obtain full posterior credible intervals and tail probabilities (e.g. P(δ > 0)) directly. Adding this would let us state, per cell, how robustly each gap clears zero rather than only its sign and magnitude.

**Defending the parallel-trends assumption explicitly.** The IRT model partly addresses the CA/CS difficulty mismatch, but reviewers will likely ask for additional defenses. Candidate extensions:
- Surface-feature matching of CA and CS subsets (length, named-entity density, per-token perplexity).
- A "neutral-surface" CS subset where answer options contain no language-marked lexical items, ruling out lexical-familiarity explanations.
- A formal sensitivity analysis quantifying how much the proficiency penalty would have to differ between CA and CS to nullify the knowledge-gap result.

**Predictive analysis of gap magnitude.** Beyond describing the gap, we could regress the estimated δ on candidate predictors — training-data share of the local language, linguistic distance from English (lang2vec / WALS), question subdomain, model family — to convert the descriptive finding into a quantitative regularity. This would turn "the gap exists" into "we can predict when and how much," and would also give a formal account of *why* Chinese is currently the only locale where the advantage surfaces in raw accuracy.

**Quantifying translation-quality effects on LocalGap.** Since CS_EN translation noise is likely making our negative LocalGap estimates more negative than they should be, a sensitivity analysis using multiple translation systems or quality-scored subsets would let us bound the true LocalGap and identify whether any additional locales would flip to positive under cleaner translation.

**Mechanistic localization.** Logit-lens and activation-patching experiments (following Wendler et al., Schut et al.) could test whether, in English-centric models, culture-specific knowledge is *retrieved* in intermediate layers under local-language queries but lost in the final language-projection stage. This would give the behavioral finding a representational basis and lift the work from evaluation to mechanism.

**Generation-based evaluation as robustness check.** Replicating the headline result with free-form generation on a subset of items would address known critiques of log-likelihood MCQ scoring (sensitivity to option ordering, surface form bias).

These extensions are natural follow-ups: any one of them could anchor a second paper, and any subset of them can be added during revision if reviewers request stronger evidence on a specific claim.

A few notes on how the findings reshape the framing, in case any of this is worth pushing back on:

- The Chinese result is now doing real explanatory work in the paper. It's not just an outlier — it's the predicted special case where the framework's two effects align rather than oppose, so the knowledge advantage surfaces in raw accuracy. I'd highlight this explicitly in the introduction as your "existence proof," because it gives readers a concrete intuition for what the masked advantage looks like when it's unmasked.
- Worth being honest in the paper that the CS_EN translation noise asymmetrically hurts the English condition (you can't have *exactly* the same item in two languages), which makes your LocalGap a slightly conservative test against the local language. I added a note on this; it actually strengthens the paper because it pre-empts an obvious reviewer objection.
- Moving every gap onto the 1PL binary MLE ability scale — rather than mixing raw-accuracy differences for GlobalGap/LocalGap with an IRT-based KnowledgeGap — makes the methodology cleaner to describe (one model, one estimator, one likelihood) and removes a class of reviewer objections about composition drift inside CA or CS. The cost is that the headline numbers are now in logits, not percentage points, so the paper has to do a little more work to give readers intuition for "how big is big." Reporting raw-accuracy gaps in an appendix table, alongside the θ-based headline numbers, is a cheap way to bridge that. The headline pattern — δ positive nearly everywhere despite LocalGap being mixed — still holds as a point estimate, and it remains the cleanest justification for the whole methodological apparatus: without the IRT analysis you'd just see "LocalGap is mostly negative" and conclude (wrongly, like prior work) that English is better. The IRT model is what lets you see through the surface to the actual knowledge effect — foreground this. Just be ready for the reviewer ask on uncertainty, which the Fisher/Schur (or NUTS refit) step in §6 is designed to answer.
